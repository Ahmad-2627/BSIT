## STAGE 1 — Managing & The Manager's Job

### 1.1 What is Management?
**Definition (Griffin):** Management = a set of activities directed at an organization's resources, aimed at achieving goals **efficiently** and **effectively**.

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Efficient** | Doing things RIGHT — minimum waste | Delivering pizza using least fuel |
| **Effective** | Doing the RIGHT things — hitting the real goal | Delivering pizza to the correct customer |

⚠️ **Trap:** You can be efficient WITHOUT being effective (1000 units made perfectly, but nobody wants them = failure). **Effectiveness comes first.**

**4 Resources:** Human, Financial, Physical, Information
**Logic:** Resources → Management Activities → Goals Achieved

---

### 1.2 Management Process — 4 Functions (POLC)
Memory: **"Please Or Leave Carefully"**

| Function | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Planning** | Deciding what/how/when | McDonald's planning 10 new branches |
| **Organizing** | Arranging people & resources | Hiring staff, setting up kitchens |
| **Leading** | Motivating & directing | Manager encouraging team |
| **Controlling** | Measuring results vs goals | Checking if sales targets were hit |

```
PLANNING → ORGANIZING → LEADING → CONTROLLING
   ↑____________ feedback loop _____________|
```
⚠️ Happens **simultaneously**, not step-by-step. Some books say "Directing" instead of Leading — same thing. ⭐*Fall 2024*

---

### 1.3 Kinds of Managers
**By Level:** Top (strategic, long-term) → Middle (tactical, manages *other managers*) → First-line (operational, manages *workers* directly).
⚠️ Trap: Middle managers manage managers, not workers.

**By Area:** Marketing, Finance, HR, Operations, IT Managers (specialists) vs **General Manager** (oversees all areas). ⭐*Fall 2025*

---

### 1.4 Mintzberg's 10 Managerial Roles
A **role** = a behavior pattern expected in a situation. Managers switch between all 10 in one day.

| Category | Roles | Core Idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Interpersonal** | Figurehead, Leader, Liaison | About people/relationships |
| **Informational** | Monitor, Disseminator, Spokesperson | Handling information |
| **Decisional** | Entrepreneur, Disturbance Handler, Resource Allocator, Negotiator | Making decisions |

⚠️ Traps: Disseminator (inside org) ≠ Spokesperson (outside org). Liaison (builds relationships) ≠ Negotiator (formal deals). Figurehead (ceremonial) ≠ Leader (motivating).

---

### 1.5 Managerial Skills — Katz's Model
Importance of each skill **changes by level** — this is why great engineers don't automatically become great managers.

```
              TECHNICAL   HUMAN   CONCEPTUAL
Top Managers     LOW       HIGH      HIGH
Middle Managers  MEDIUM    HIGH      MEDIUM
First-Line       HIGH      HIGH      LOW
```
- **Technical** ↓ decreases going up · **Conceptual** ↑ increases going up · **Human** = always equally important
⚠️ Never write "human skill less important at top" — it never decreases. ⭐*Fall 2025*

---

### 1.6 Omnipotent vs Symbolic View
*"How much control does a manager really have?"*

| | Omnipotent View | Symbolic View |
|---|---|---|
| Belief | Manager is FULLY responsible | Manager has LIMITED real control |
| Also called | Rational view | External constraint view |
| Example | VW fires CEO after scandal | COVID collapsed good businesses regardless of manager skill |

**Reality:** Somewhere in the middle — managers have real but limited influence. ⭐*Fall 2024*

---

### 1.7 Nature of Managerial Work
Research (Mintzberg, Kotter) shows real managerial work is:
1. **Fast-paced & relentless** — no "finished" state
2. **Highly varied** — jumps between totally different topics
3. **Fragmented** — most activities last **under 9 minutes**
4. **Primarily verbal** — 70–90% of time in talking, not writing
5. **Networking-centered** — needs a wide web of contacts
6. **More reactive than proactive** — responds to problems constantly

⚠️ This is *normal*, not a sign of poor management.

---

### 1.8 Workforce Diversity
**Definition (Robbins):** The ways people in an organization differ from and resemble one another.

- **Surface-level** (visible: age, gender, race) vs **Deep-level** (hidden: values, personality) — deep-level conflicts are often *harder* to spot and fix.
- **Diversity** (having different people) ≠ **Equality** (fair treatment) ≠ **Inclusion** (feeling valued) — three different things.
- Diversity alone does NOT automatically improve performance — it must be *managed well*.

⚠️ *Fall 2024, Spring 2025* — commonly asked directly.

## STAGE 2 — Ethical & Social Environment

### 2.1 What is Ethics & Why it Matters
**Definition (Robbins):** Rules and principles defining right and wrong conduct in an organization.
**Key rule:** Legal ≠ always ethical (e.g., legally paying minimum wage while treating workers badly).

**4 Approaches to judge ethics:**
| Approach | Logic |
|---|---|
| Utilitarian | Greatest good for the greatest number |
| Rights | Respect basic human rights (privacy, dignity) |
| Justice | Treat people fairly & consistently |
| Integrative | Universal principles + local norms |

**Newspaper Test:** *"Would I be okay if this decision was on tomorrow's front page?"*

---

### 2.2 Individual Ethics in Organizations
**3 factors shape a person's ethics at work:**

**1) Kohlberg's Stages of Moral Development** (Pre-conventional → Conventional → Post-conventional)
| Level | Logic |
|---|---|
| Pre-conventional (1–2) | "I'll be punished" / "What benefits me" |
| Conventional (3–4) | "Be seen as good" / "Rules must be followed" — *most adults are here* |
| Post-conventional (5–6) | "Unjust rules should be challenged" / "I follow my conscience" |

**2) Personal Values & Personality**
- **Ego strength** = ability to stick to ethics under pressure
- **Locus of control**: Internal ("I control my actions" → more ethical) vs External ("outside forces control me" → blames others)

**3) Organizational Factors:** culture, authority pressure (Milgram effect), peer norms, and **ethical climate** (Instrumental = self-interest, Caring = wellbeing, Rules-based = compliance).

⚠️ Locus of control ≠ ego strength — different concepts. Good personal values alone can still be overridden by org pressure.

---

### 2.3 Emerging Ethical Issues
| Issue | Core Point |
|---|---|
| **Privacy & Surveillance** | Monitoring (email, GPS, keystrokes) vs employee's right to privacy |
| **Technology & AI Ethics** | Data privacy, algorithmic bias (Amazon's biased hiring AI, scrapped 2018), job loss from automation, deepfakes |
| **Whistleblowing** | Reporting wrongdoing internally or externally — the *highest form of loyalty* (loyalty to truth). Manager must never retaliate. |
| **Sexual Harassment** | **Quid pro quo** (job benefit for sexual favor) vs **Hostile work environment** (ongoing offensive behavior) — know both types |
| **Discrimination** | Age, gender, race, religion, disability, appearance |
| **Environmental Ethics** | Carbon footprint, **greenwashing** (fake green claims), sustainability |

⚠️ Quid pro quo ≠ hostile environment. Whistleblowing = loyalty, not disloyalty. ⭐*Fall 2025 (appeared twice)*

---

### 2.4 Social Responsibility (CSR)
**Two Views:**
| | Classical (Friedman) | Socioeconomic (Davis) |
|---|---|---|
| Belief | Business exists ONLY to profit | Business has duties beyond profit |

**4 Stances (least → most responsible):** Obstructionist → Defensive → Accommodative → Proactive

**Arguments For:** better image, avoids regulation, long-term loyalty.
**Arguments Against:** dilutes profit, managers aren't social experts, unfair to costs.

**Shared Value (Porter):** Creating profit *and* social value together (e.g., helping farmers grow better crops → better income for them + better raw material for company).

⚠️ CSR ≠ only donations — includes environment, labor, governance. Ethical companies are NOT automatically less profitable.

## STAGE 3 — Environment & Culture of Management

### 3.1 Why Environment Matters
Organizations are **open systems** — constantly affected by their surroundings.
**Uncertainty = Change (stable/dynamic) × Complexity (simple/complex).** Local restaurant = low uncertainty. Global tech firm = high uncertainty.

### 3.2 External Environment
- **General Environment (PESTLE)** — affects *all* businesses: Political-Legal, Economic, Sociocultural, Technological, Legal, Environmental.
- **Task Environment** — affects *your* business directly: Competitors, Customers, Suppliers, Regulators, Partners.

⚠️ Customers = Task environment, NOT general. ⭐*Spring 2025*

### 3.3 Internal Environment
4 components (manager has real control here): **Owners** → set vision · **Board of Directors** → oversight ("right things?") vs **Management** does the work ("things right?") · **Employees** → execute plans · **Culture** → covered next.
⚠️ Board ≠ Management — different groups.

### 3.4 Organization–Environment Relationship
- **Adapting** (changing yourself): info management, strategic response, M&A, flexible structures
- **Influencing** (changing environment): PR/advertising, lobbying, trade associations, bold strategic moves
⚠️ Merger = two become one. Alliance = stay separate.

### 3.5 Organizational Culture
*"Culture eats strategy for breakfast" — Drucker*
**Definition:** Shared values, beliefs, and behaviors that guide how members act.

**7 Dimensions:** Innovation, Risk-taking, Attention to detail, Outcome orientation, People orientation, Team orientation, Aggressiveness, Stability.

**Strong culture** = shared values, consistent behavior, higher performance (Apple, Toyota) vs **Weak culture** = fragmented, inconsistent.
**Maintained by:** socialization, stories, rituals, symbols, language.

⚠️ Strong ≠ always good (must also be ethical). Culture = hardest/slowest thing to change. ⭐*Spring 2025*

### 3.6 Scientific Management (Taylor)
**Father:** Frederick **Winslow** Taylor (early 1900s) — replaced guesswork with science.
**4 Principles:** Develop science for each task · Scientifically select & train workers · Cooperate with workers · Divide work between managers (plan) and workers (execute).
Also: **piece-rate pay** (pay by output), **Gilbreth** (motion study), **Gantt** (Gantt chart).

**Criticism:** "Treats workers like machines" — ignores creativity and social needs. Works well for repetitive tasks, fails for creative/knowledge work.
⚠️ Gantt chart = Henry Gantt, NOT Taylor. ⭐*Spring 2025*

### 3.7 Going Global — 6 Entry Strategies
Low commitment → High commitment:
**Export/Import → Licensing (sell IP rights, earn royalty) → Franchising (sell whole business system, e.g. McDonald's) → Strategic Alliance (cooperate, stay separate) → Joint Venture (create a new shared company) → FDI (own operations directly — highest risk/control/commitment; Greenfield = build new, Acquisition = buy existing).**

⚠️ Biggest trap: **Licensing (just IP) ≠ Franchising (whole system + training)**. Alliance ≠ JV (JV creates a new company). ⭐*Fall 2024, Spring 2025*

## STAGE 4 — Planning & Decision Making

### 4.1–4.2 Planning & Its Benefits
**Definition:** Setting goals and deciding how to achieve them.
**Benefits:** minimizes waste, gives direction, reduces uncertainty, aids coordination, sets control standards, sets priorities. ⭐*Spring 2025*

### 4.3 Goals & SMART
| Level | Goal Type | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Top | Strategic | 3–5 years |
| Middle | Tactical | 1–2 years |
| First-line | Operational | Daily/weekly |

**SMART** = Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

### 4.4 Levels & Types of Plans
Strategic (top) → Tactical (middle) → Operational (first-line).
**Single-use plan** (one-time) vs **Standing plan** (repeated) → Standing plans = **Policies** (guidelines), **Procedures** (step-by-step), **Rules** (must/must-not).

### 4.5 Levels of Strategy
**Corporate Strategy** ("What businesses should we be in?") — set by top mgmt/board.
- **Growth** (expand — via Concentration, Vertical Integration, or Diversification) · **Stability** (stay same) · **Retrenchment** (cut back)

**Business Strategy** ("How do we compete?") — Porter's 3 Generic Strategies:
| Strategy | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost Leadership** | Be the cheapest, broad market | Walmart |
| **Differentiation** | Be unique, broad market | Apple |
| **Focus** | Narrow segment (cost or differentiation focus) | Rolls Royce |

⚠️ "Stuck in the middle" = trying to be both cost leader AND differentiator = performs poorly at both.

**Functional Strategy** ("How does each department support it?") — must align with the above two.

### 4.6–4.8 Decision Making & Types
**Definition:** Identifying and selecting a course of action.
- **Programmed** (routine, clear solution) vs **Non-programmed** (unique, complex)
- **Certainty** (outcome known) vs **Risk** (probabilities estimable) vs **Uncertainty** (unpredictable) — most real decisions happen under Risk/Uncertainty.

### 4.7 Decision Making Process — 8 Steps ⭐*Fall 2024, Fall 2025*
```
1. Recognize the problem
2. Identify decision criteria
3. Allocate weights to criteria
4. Develop alternatives
5. Evaluate alternatives
6. Select best alternative
7. Implement decision
8. Evaluate effectiveness
```

### 4.9 Classical vs Administrative Model
| | Classical (Normative) | Administrative (Herbert Simon) |
|---|---|---|
| Info | Complete & perfect | Limited & imperfect |
| Decision maker | Perfectly rational | **Bounded rationality** (limited by time, info, brainpower) |
| Goal | **Maximize** — find the absolute best | **Satisfice** — find "good enough" |
| Reality | Ideal/theoretical | Realistic/practical |

Administrative model also includes **Intuition** — quick gut-feeling decisions from years of pattern-recognition experience (not random guessing).
⚠️ Satisficing ≠ lazy — it's the practical response to real-world limits.

### 4.10 Behavioral Aspects of Decision Making
- **Heuristics** [mental shortcuts]: Availability (judging by what's easy to recall), Representativeness (judging by similarity to a known case), Anchoring (over-relying on first info received)
- **Framing Effect** — same facts, different wording, different decision ("90% survival" feels better than "10% death rate")
- **Escalation of Commitment** — "throwing good money after bad" because of **sunk cost** [money already spent, unrecoverable] — rational response is to ignore sunk costs
- **Risk Propensity** — Risk taker (bold, uncertain outcomes) vs Risk avoider (prefers certainty)

### 4.11 Group Decision-Making Techniques
| Technique | Face-to-face? | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Brainstorming** | Yes | No criticism during idea generation, quantity over quality |
| **Devil's Advocacy** | Yes | One person *assigned* to challenge every idea |
| **Nominal Group (NGT)** | Yes (later) | Silent individual writing → share → discuss → private vote |
| **Delphi Technique** | **No** | Anonymous expert questionnaires, repeated until consensus — slow but honest |

⚠️ Delphi's defining feature = NO face-to-face. NGT starts individually. Brainstorming bans criticism only *during* generation.

## STAGE 5 — The Organizing Process

### 5.1 What is Organizing
Deciding **who does what**, with what resources, and how it all fits together. 5 key decisions: Designing jobs → Grouping jobs → Reporting relationships → Distributing authority → Coordinating activities.

### 5.2 Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham)
**5 core characteristics → 3 psychological states → motivation/satisfaction/performance:**

| Characteristic | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Skill Variety | Uses different skills/talents |
| Task Identity | Completes a whole piece of work, not a fragment |
| Task Significance | Work meaningfully impacts others' lives |
| Autonomy | Freedom to decide how/when to work |
| Feedback | Job itself tells you how well you're doing |

**MPS Formula:**
```
MPS = [(Skill Variety + Task Identity + Task Significance) ÷ 3] × Autonomy × Feedback
```
⚠️ Autonomy and Feedback are *multiplied* — if either is zero, MPS = **zero**, no matter how good the rest is.

### 5.3 Job Description vs Job Specification
| Job Description | Job Specification |
|---|---|
| Defines the **job** (duties) | Defines the **person needed** (qualifications) |
| "What will they DO?" | "What kind of person do we NEED?" |

⭐*Fall 2025* — very commonly asked short question.

### 5.4 Departmentalization — 5 Bases
| Basis | Groups by | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | Business function (marketing, finance...) | Most common, but siloed [isolated] |
| **Product** | Product line | Unilever's Food / Home Care / Personal Care divisions |
| **Customer** | Customer type | HBL's Corporate/Retail/Islamic banking |
| **Geographic** | Region | Daraz's Punjab/Sindh/KPK regions |
| **Process** | Workflow stage | Garment factory: Cutting → Stitching → QC → Packaging |

⚠️ Real organizations often combine multiple bases — no single basis is universally best.

### 5.5 Reporting Relationships
- **Chain of Command** — unbroken line of authority top to bottom; everyone has one clear boss.
- **Span of Control** — how many people one manager supervises.

| Narrow Span | Wide Span |
|---|---|
| Few people per manager → Tall structure, more levels | Many people per manager → Flat structure, fewer levels |
| Close supervision, costly | More employee autonomy, cheaper |
| Best for complex work | Best for routine work |

### 5.6 Centralization vs Decentralization
**Centralization** = decisions stay at the top (fast consistency, slower response — good for crisis/small orgs).
**Decentralization** = decisions pushed down (faster, more local fit — good for large/diverse orgs).
⚠️ Neither is universally better — depends on context. ⭐*Fall 2024*

### 5.7 Authority, Responsibility, Accountability & Delegation
| Concept | Meaning | Flows |
|---|---|---|
| **Authority** | Formal right of a *position* to decide/act | Downward |
| **Responsibility** | Obligation to perform assigned work | Upward |
| **Accountability** | Obligation to *report/explain* results — cannot be delegated away | Upward |

**Golden Rule:** Authority = Responsibility = Accountability (must be balanced — giving one without the others creates dysfunction).

**Delegation (3 steps):** Assign responsibility → Grant authority → Create accountability.
Managers often fail to delegate due to: "I can do it better myself," fear of losing control, fear of being outperformed, lack of trust, "no time to explain."

**Line vs Staff vs Functional Authority:**
- **Line** = direct command (production manager → workers)
- **Staff** = advisory only, cannot order (HR advising, not commanding)
- **Functional** = limited command power in a specific specialty area (safety officer can halt unsafe operations anywhere)

### 5.8 Coordinating Activities
3 techniques: **Managerial Hierarchy** (escalate to common boss), **Rules & Procedures** (automatic coordination), **Liaison Roles** (dedicated coordinator, task force, or integrating department).

**Mechanistic vs Organic Structure:**
| | Mechanistic | Organic |
|---|---|---|
| Best for | Stable, simple environment | Dynamic, complex environment |
| Traits | Rigid, centralized, tall, rule-heavy | Flexible, decentralized, flat, judgment-guided |
| Example | Military, traditional banks | Startups, R&D firms |

⚠️ Mismatch = failure (Kodak stayed mechanistic in a fast-changing industry → bankrupt). Neither is "always better" — structure must fit environment.

## STAGE 6 — Leadership & Influence

### 6.1 Leadership vs Management
| Management | Leadership |
|---|---|
| Systems, processes, order | People, inspiration, vision |
| Power from **position** | Power from personal **influence/trust** |
| "How do we do this right?" | "What should we be doing?" |

Best managers are also strong leaders — organizations need both.

### 6.2 Key Leader Characteristics
Drive, Motivation to lead, Honesty & integrity, Self-confidence, Intelligence, Knowledge of business (+ emotional intelligence, charisma, adaptability). ⭐*Fall 2025*

### 6.3 Trait Approach
Oldest theory — assumes leaders are **born** with traits (intelligence, confidence, integrity). Largely rejected today — leadership can be learned; ignores followers and situations.

### 6.4 Behavioral Approaches
| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Iowa (Lewin)** | 3 styles: Autocratic (leader decides alone), Democratic (team involved), Laissez-faire (hands-off) |
| **Ohio State** | 2 dimensions: Initiating Structure (task-focused) & Consideration (people-focused) — independent of each other |
| **Michigan** | Same 2 dimensions, called Job-centered & Employee-centered |
| **Managerial Grid (Blake & Mouton)** | Plots Concern for People vs Concern for Task; **(9,9) Team Management = ideal** |

### 6.5 Situational/Contingency Approaches
- **Fiedler's Model** — match leader style (task- vs relationship-oriented) to situational favorableness (leader-member relations, task structure, position power).
- **Path-Goal (House)** — leader clears the path for followers: Directive, Supportive, Participative, Achievement-oriented (choose based on task/employee).
- **Hersey & Blanchard** — match style to **follower readiness**: R1→Telling, R2→Selling, R3→Participating, R4→Delegating.

### 6.6 Transformational vs Transactional Leadership
| Transactional | Transformational |
|---|---|
| Exchange: "do this, get that" | Inspires a shared vision |
| Maintains current systems | Creates fundamental change |

**4 Components of Transformational (Bass):** Idealized influence (charisma), Inspirational motivation, Intellectual stimulation, Individualized consideration.
⚠️ Best organizations need BOTH types.

### 6.7 Political Behavior in Organizations
**Definition:** Informal influence activities (outside your formal role) to gain advantage.
**Types:** Networking, Image building, Ingratiation [flattery to gain favor], Coalition building, Information control, Scapegoating.
Not always bad — positive politics (networking, relationship-building) can help; negative politics harms the organization. Reduce it with transparency, objective evaluation, clear goals.

### 6.8 Ensuring Good Management Practices ⭐*Fall 2024 & Spring 2025 (asked both times)*
Framework to answer this exact question: Apply **POLC** → build a strong ethical **culture** → **develop people** (train, delegate, give feedback) → **communicate** effectively → maintain **ethical standards** (newspaper test) → continuously **scan and adapt** to the environment.

## STAGE 7 — Managing Change & Innovation

### 7.1 Why Change Happens
Change is inevitable — Kodak, Nokia, Blockbuster all had good management but failed to manage *change*.
**Planned change** (deliberate) vs **Reactive change** (response to unexpected events, e.g., COVID pivot to online).

### 7.2 Forces for Change
**External:** Technological change, Economic changes, Competition, Social/Political changes, International forces.
**Internal:** Declining performance, Changing workforce, New equipment/technology, Employee attitudes/morale.
⭐*Fall 2025*

### 7.3 Steps in the Change Process
**Lewin's 3-Step Model:**
```
UNFREEZE (build awareness/motivation) → CHANGE (implement, train, support) → REFREEZE (make it the new normal, reward adoption)
```

**Kotter's 8-Step Model** (for larger change):
```
1. Create urgency        5. Empower broad-based action
2. Build guiding coalition   6. Generate short-term wins
3. Develop vision & strategy 7. Consolidate gains
4. Communicate the vision    8. Anchor changes in culture
```
Short-term wins (step 6) matter because change is exhausting — people need visible early progress or they give up.

### 7.4 Resistance to Change
**Individual:** fear of unknown, habit, loss of security, economic concerns, selective information processing.
**Organizational:** structural inertia, group inertia, threat to expertise, threat to established power, resource limits.
⚠️ Resistance is normal — not a sign of failure.

### 7.5 Overcoming Resistance (Kotter & Schlesinger)
From most positive to most damaging:
**Education & Communication → Participation & Involvement → Facilitation & Support → Negotiation & Agreement → Manipulation & Co-optation → Coercion (last resort only).**

### 7.6 The Innovation Process
**Types:** Product, Process, Business model, Radical (breakthrough) vs Incremental (small ongoing improvements).
**Stages:** Development → Application → Launch → Growth → Maturity → Decline.
**What fosters innovation:** creative/psychologically-safe culture, slack resources [spare time/budget], **intrapreneurship** [employees acting like entrepreneurs inside the company — e.g. Gmail and Post-it Notes started this way], diverse teams, rewarding even failed experiments, leadership support.

## STAGE 8 — The Controlling Process

### 8.1 What is Control & Its Purpose
**Definition (Griffin):** Regulating activities so performance stays within acceptable limits.
**Simple logic:** Set target → Measure actual → Compare → Fix the gap.
⚠️ Control is NOT punishment — it's about measuring, monitoring, and correcting.

### 8.2 Steps in the Control Process ⭐
```
1. Establish Standards  (quantitative, qualitative, time, or cost targets)
2. Measure Actual Performance  (observation, reports, electronic monitoring)
3. Compare Performance with Standards  (is the gap significant?)
4. Take Corrective Action  → do nothing / correct the deviation / revise the standard
```
⚠️ Sometimes the *standard* is wrong, not the performance — good managers tell the difference.

### 8.3 Types of Control (by Timing)
| Type | When | Focus | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Feedforward** | Before work | Inputs — prevents problems | Screening job applicants, inspecting raw materials |
| **Concurrent** | During work | Process — fixes in real time | Supervisor correcting technique as it happens |
| **Feedback** | After work | Outputs — learn & improve | Annual performance review (most common but most reactive) |

### 8.3B Control by Organizational Level
- **Operational** (first-line, daily) — production schedules, quality checklists
- **Structural** — **Bureaucratic control** (rules/policies) vs **Clan control** (shared values/culture guide behavior)
- **Strategic** (top mgmt, long-term) — is the overall strategy still working? Tools: Balanced Scorecard, strategic reviews, benchmarking.

### 8.4 Total Quality Management (TQM) ⭐*Fall 2024*
**Definition:** Organization-wide commitment to continuous improvement, quality = everyone's job.
**4 Principles:** Customer Focus (customer defines quality) · Continuous Improvement (**Kaizen**) · Employee Involvement (quality circles) · Process Focus (fix the process, not the person).
**Tools:** Benchmarking, Statistical Process Control, Six Sigma, ISO 9000.
⚠️ TQM ≠ only the QC department's job; it's continuous, never a one-time project.

### 8.5 Managing Productivity
```
Productivity = Outputs ÷ Inputs
```
Types: Labor productivity, Capital productivity, Total factor productivity.
**Improve via:** technology investment, employee training, process improvement, motivation, better management.
⚠️ Common myth: faster ≠ lower quality — improving quality often *increases* productivity (less rework/waste — Toyota example).

### 8.6 Performance Appraisal ⭐*Spring 2025*
**Definition:** Formal process of evaluating an employee's work against standards, and communicating results.
**Purpose:** administrative decisions (raises/promotions), development, motivation, feedback, legal protection, goal alignment.

**Methods:** Rating scales · **360-degree feedback** (from supervisor + peers + subordinates + self) · **MBO** (employee & manager jointly set goals) · **BARS** (behavior-anchored scale).

**Common Problems:** Halo effect (one good trait colors everything), Horn effect (one bad trait colors everything), Recency bias (recent events dominate), Central tendency (rating everyone "average"), Leniency bias (rating everyone too high).
